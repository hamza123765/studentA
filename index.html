Don Norman · HCI Philosopher

Don Norman is a pioneer in the field of Human–Computer Interaction, known for his work on user-centered design, usability, and cognitive engineering. His Action Cycle (seven stages of interaction) and gulfs of execution and evaluation are foundational frameworks for analyzing interaction (Dix et al., Ch.9).

Three Pillars of Interaction Analysis

Lab & Assignment 01 focuses on three complementary models for understanding human-computer interaction:

1. Norman's Action Cycle

What it is: A cognitive framework describing how users interact with systems through seven stages: goal, plan, specify, execute, perceive, interpret, evaluate.

Key concepts: Gulf of execution (mismatch between user intentions and allowable actions), Gulf of evaluation (mismatch between system state and user understanding).

When to use: Identifying usability problems, understanding user errors, designing for learnability.

2. GOMS (Card, Moran, Newell, 1983)

What it is: An engineering model that predicts expert, error-free performance. Components: Goals, Operators, Methods, Selection rules.

Key concepts: Hierarchical task decomposition, quantifiable operators, method selection based on context.

When to use: Comparing efficiency of different interfaces, predicting task completion time for routine tasks.

3. KLM (Keystroke-Level Model)

What it is: A simplified GOMS for rapid execution-time prediction using six operators: K (keystroke), P (point), H (home), D (draw), M (mental), R (response).

Key concepts: Operator time values (e.g., K = 0.2s, P = 1.1s, M = 1.35s), no modeling of planning or interpretation.

When to use: Quick usability benchmarks, comparing micro-interactions, expert performance prediction.

Discussion Examples (Week 01–04)

Toggle Switch Design

Norman's Gulf of Execution: User cannot determine if toggle is on/off without visual feedback. KLM prediction: [M][P][K] = ~2.65s per tap.

Automatic Syringe

GOMS analysis: GOAL: ADMINISTER-DOSE → METHOD: PRESS-PLUNGER → OPERATOR: Apply force. KLM: Physical operators only; no modeling of precision requirements.

Who Paid Problem

Norman's Gulf of Evaluation: System provides no feedback about who paid; user cannot evaluate goal completion. GOMS does not model this evaluation gap.
